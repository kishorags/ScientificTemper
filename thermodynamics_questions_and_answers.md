# Scientific Temper Competition - Thermodynamics Sample Questions and Answers

## For High School Students

This document contains sample questions and answers focused on **thermodynamics** — the science of heat, energy, and work. These questions are designed to test the approach and reasoning ability of students regarding everyday phenomena that involve thermodynamic principles. Students are expected to identify the relevant scientific concepts and reason through the solution step-by-step.

---

## Evaluation Criteria

When evaluating answers, judges emphasize the following (in order of importance):

1. **Understanding of Basic Scientific Principles:** Does the student demonstrate knowledge of fundamental thermodynamic concepts relevant to the question?
2. **Approach and Reasoning:** Does the student show a logical, step-by-step thought process? How did they arrive at their answer?
3. **Operations and Logic:** Are the reasoning steps clear, and is the logic for choosing a particular solution well explained?
4. **Brevity and Focus:** Are answers concise and to the point, avoiding unnecessary information?
5. **Originality:** Does the student offer creative or original ideas while remaining scientifically accurate?

> **Note for students:** Judges care more about *how you think* than *what you know*. A partially correct answer with excellent reasoning will score higher than a correct answer with no explanation of approach.

## Question 1: Why does a hot cup of tea cool down over time?

**Reasoning Approach:**
1. *What is happening to the tea?* — It is losing heat and approaching room temperature.
2. *How is heat being lost?* — Through multiple mechanisms: conduction, convection, radiation, and evaporation.
3. *What principle governs the direction of heat flow?* — The second law of thermodynamics — heat flows spontaneously from hot to cold.

**Answer:**
A hot cup of tea cools down because heat flows from a hotter body (tea) to cooler surroundings until **thermal equilibrium** is reached. This is governed by the **second law of thermodynamics**.

- **Conduction:** Heat transfers through the cup wall to the surrounding air molecules in direct contact.
- **Convection:** Warm air near the cup rises and is replaced by cooler air, carrying heat away.
- **Radiation:** The hot tea emits infrared radiation, transferring energy to the surroundings.
- **Evaporation:** Water molecules at the surface with enough kinetic energy escape as vapor, carrying away latent heat and cooling the liquid.

The rate of cooling depends on the temperature difference between the tea and surroundings, described by **Newton's law of cooling**: dT/dt ∝ (T - Tₛ), where T is the tea's temperature and Tₛ is the surrounding temperature.

**Key Concepts:** Second law of thermodynamics, thermal equilibrium, conduction, convection, radiation, evaporation, Newton's law of cooling

## Question 2: Why does a bicycle tire feel warm after pumping air into it?

**Reasoning Approach:**
1. *What is happening when we pump air?* — Air is being compressed into a smaller volume.
2. *What happens when a gas is compressed?* — Work is done on the gas, increasing its internal energy.
3. *What does increased internal energy mean?* — Higher temperature.

**Answer:**
The tire feels warm because compressing air increases its temperature — an application of the **first law of thermodynamics** (ΔU = Q - W).

- When you pump air, you do **work on the gas**, compressing it into the tire. This work increases the internal energy of the gas molecules.
- Internal energy is related to the kinetic energy of gas molecules — higher internal energy means faster-moving molecules and higher temperature.
- The process approximates **adiabatic compression** (rapid enough that little heat escapes during pumping), described by: T₁V₁^(γ-1) = T₂V₂^(γ-1), where γ is the heat capacity ratio.
- The warm compressed air then heats the tire walls through conduction.

**Key Concepts:** First law of thermodynamics, adiabatic compression, internal energy, work-energy relationship

## Question 3: Why does blowing on hot food cool it down?

**Reasoning Approach:**
1. *What is happening at the food surface?* — Hot food is surrounded by a thin layer of warm, humid air.
2. *What does blowing do?* — It removes that warm air layer and replaces it with cooler, drier air.
3. *How does this affect heat loss?* — It increases both convective heat loss and evaporation rate.

**Answer:**
Blowing on hot food cools it by enhancing **forced convection** and **evaporation**:

- Hot food heats a thin boundary layer of air near its surface. In still conditions, this warm layer acts as an insulator, slowing further heat loss.
- **Blowing removes this boundary layer**, exposing food to cooler air with a larger temperature difference, increasing the rate of heat transfer.
- Blowing also replaces humid air (saturated with water vapor from the food) with drier air, which **accelerates evaporation**. Each gram of water that evaporates removes ~2,260 joules of heat from the food.
- The combined effect of increased convection and evaporation makes cooling significantly faster than natural cooling.

**Key Concepts:** Forced convection, boundary layer, evaporative cooling, latent heat of vaporization

## Question 4: Why do we feel warm when we rub our hands together?

**Reasoning Approach:**
1. *What is happening during rubbing?* — Two surfaces are moving against each other.
2. *What opposes this motion?* — Friction between the skin surfaces.
3. *Where does the kinetic energy go?* — It is converted into thermal energy.

**Answer:**
Rubbing hands together converts **mechanical energy into thermal energy** through friction — a direct demonstration of the **first law of thermodynamics** and conservation of energy.

- Friction between skin surfaces opposes relative motion. The work done against friction is converted into heat: W_friction = f × d, where f is the friction force and d is the distance rubbed.
- At the microscopic level, surface molecules interact and their vibrational energy increases, raising the temperature.
- This is the same principle that James Joule demonstrated experimentally — establishing that mechanical work and heat are equivalent forms of energy (the **mechanical equivalent of heat**, 1 calorie ≈ 4.186 joules).

**Key Concepts:** Conservation of energy, friction, mechanical equivalent of heat, first law of thermodynamics

## Question 5: Why does water boil at a lower temperature on a mountain top?

**Reasoning Approach:**
1. *What determines the boiling point of water?* — The temperature at which vapor pressure equals atmospheric pressure.
2. *What is different on a mountain?* — Atmospheric pressure is lower at higher altitudes.
3. *How does lower pressure affect boiling?* — Less pressure means the liquid needs less energy to form vapor bubbles.

**Answer:**
Water boils at a lower temperature on mountains because **atmospheric pressure decreases with altitude**, and boiling point depends on external pressure.

- Boiling occurs when a liquid's **vapor pressure equals the atmospheric pressure** above it. At sea level (1 atm), water boils at 100°C.
- At higher altitudes, the atmosphere is thinner and exerts less pressure. For example, at the top of Mount Everest (~8,849 m), pressure is about 0.34 atm, and water boils at approximately 70°C.
- The relationship between pressure and boiling point is described by the **Clausius-Clapeyron equation**: dP/dT = L/(TΔV), where L is latent heat and ΔV is the volume change during vaporization.
- This is why pressure cookers are especially useful at high altitudes — they restore the higher pressure needed to raise the boiling point and cook food properly.

**Key Concepts:** Vapor pressure, boiling point, atmospheric pressure, Clausius-Clapeyron equation

## Question 6: Why does an air conditioner cool a room but heat the outside?

**Reasoning Approach:**
1. *What does an AC do?* — It moves heat from inside a room to outside.
2. *Can heat flow from cold to hot spontaneously?* — No, the second law of thermodynamics forbids this.
3. *How does the AC overcome this?* — By doing external work (using electrical energy) to force heat transfer.

**Answer:**
An air conditioner is a **heat pump** that transfers heat from a cooler space (room) to a warmer space (outside) by consuming electrical work — consistent with the **second law of thermodynamics**.

- A refrigerant fluid circulates through a cycle of compression and expansion:
  - **Inside (evaporator):** Refrigerant evaporates at low pressure, absorbing heat from room air (Qcold).
  - **Compressor:** Does work (W) on the refrigerant, compressing it and raising its temperature.
  - **Outside (condenser):** Hot, compressed refrigerant releases heat to outside air (Qhot).
- By conservation of energy: **Qhot = Qcold + W** — the heat ejected outside equals the heat absorbed inside plus the work input.
- This is why the total heat outside always exceeds the cooling inside — the AC adds work energy to the system.

**Key Concepts:** Second law of thermodynamics, heat pump cycle, conservation of energy, refrigeration cycle

## Question 7: Why does a thermos flask keep hot drinks hot and cold drinks cold?

**Reasoning Approach:**
1. *What would cause a drink to change temperature?* — Heat transfer to or from surroundings.
2. *What modes of heat transfer exist?* — Conduction, convection, and radiation.
3. *How does the thermos minimize all three?* — Each design feature targets a specific mode.

**Answer:**
A thermos flask minimizes all three modes of heat transfer, keeping contents at their original temperature:

- **Conduction minimized:** A vacuum between the inner and outer walls eliminates conduction — heat cannot travel through empty space by molecular collisions.
- **Convection minimized:** The vacuum also eliminates convective currents — no air or fluid exists between the walls to carry heat.
- **Radiation minimized:** The inner walls are coated with a reflective (silvered) surface that reflects infrared radiation back, reducing radiative heat loss.
- **Additional features:** A tight stopper prevents heat loss through evaporation and convection at the opening.

The thermos does not "know" whether the contents are hot or cold — it simply slows heat exchange in both directions. The **zeroth law of thermodynamics** tells us that without heat transfer, the contents maintain their temperature.

**Key Concepts:** Conduction, convection, radiation, vacuum insulation, zeroth law of thermodynamics

## Question 8: Why does dew form on grass in the early morning?

**Reasoning Approach:**
1. *What is dew?* — Tiny water droplets on surfaces.
2. *Where does the water come from?* — Water vapor already present in the air.
3. *Why does vapor become liquid?* — The air cools below its dew point temperature, and vapor condenses.

**Answer:**
Dew forms because the ground and objects near it cool overnight through **radiative cooling**, causing water vapor in the air to **condense**:

- At night, the ground radiates infrared energy into the sky and cools. Grass, being thin with a large surface area, cools especially fast.
- Air in contact with the cool grass loses heat. When air temperature drops to the **dew point** — the temperature at which it becomes saturated (100% relative humidity) — water vapor condenses into liquid droplets.
- Condensation releases **latent heat** (~2,260 J/g), which slightly warms the surroundings, but the continued radiative cooling drives ongoing dew formation.
- Dew forms preferentially on clear, calm nights because clouds reflect radiation back to the ground, and wind mixes warmer air down, both preventing sufficient cooling.

**Key Concepts:** Radiative cooling, dew point, condensation, latent heat, phase change

## Question 9: Why do we wrap ice cream in newspaper to keep it from melting?

**Reasoning Approach:**
1. *Why does ice cream melt?* — It absorbs heat from the warmer surroundings.
2. *How can we slow this?* — By reducing heat transfer from the environment to the ice cream.
3. *Why newspaper specifically?* — It has properties that make it a good thermal insulator.

**Answer:**
Newspaper acts as a **thermal insulator**, slowing the flow of heat from the warm surroundings to the cold ice cream:

- Newspaper contains many tiny air pockets trapped between and within its fibers. Air has very low **thermal conductivity** (~0.025 W/m·K), making it an excellent insulator.
- These air pockets reduce **conduction** — heat must pass through many alternating layers of fiber and air, each boundary resisting heat flow.
- The layers also suppress **convection** by trapping air in small spaces, preventing warm air currents from directly reaching the ice cream surface.
- The principle is the same used in double-glazed windows, wool clothing, and styrofoam — **trapped air is one of nature's best insulators**.

**Key Concepts:** Thermal conductivity, insulation, conduction, convection, trapped air as insulator

## Question 10: Why does a metal spoon in hot soup get hot quickly while a wooden spoon does not?

**Reasoning Approach:**
1. *Both spoons are in the same hot soup — why different results?* — The material matters.
2. *What property determines how fast heat travels through a material?* — Thermal conductivity.
3. *Why is metal a better conductor?* — Free electrons in metals transfer energy rapidly.

**Answer:**
Metal gets hot quickly because it has much higher **thermal conductivity** than wood:

- **Metals** (e.g., stainless steel ~16 W/m·K, aluminum ~205 W/m·K) have free electrons that rapidly transfer kinetic energy along the material. Heat travels quickly from the submerged end to the handle.
- **Wood** (~0.1–0.2 W/m·K) lacks free electrons and has a porous, fibrous structure with trapped air pockets. Heat transfers slowly through molecular vibrations alone.
- The ratio of thermal conductivities means heat travels roughly 100–1000 times faster through metal than wood.
- This is also why metal feels cold to the touch at room temperature — it rapidly conducts heat away from your warm skin, while wood does not.

**Key Concepts:** Thermal conductivity, free electron model, heat conduction, material properties

## Tips for Approaching Thermodynamics Questions

1. **Identify the System and Surroundings:** Define what is being studied and what is external to it. This is the first step in any thermodynamics problem.

2. **Think About Energy Flow:** Ask — where is energy coming from, where is it going, and in what form (heat, work, radiation)?

3. **Apply the Laws of Thermodynamics:**
   - **Zeroth Law:** If two systems are each in thermal equilibrium with a third, they are in equilibrium with each other (defines temperature).
   - **First Law:** Energy is conserved — ΔU = Q - W (change in internal energy = heat added minus work done).
   - **Second Law:** Heat flows spontaneously from hot to cold, never the reverse without external work. Entropy of an isolated system always increases.
   - **Third Law:** As temperature approaches absolute zero, entropy approaches a minimum.

4. **Consider All Heat Transfer Modes:** For any temperature change, ask — is heat moving by conduction, convection, radiation, or a combination?

5. **Look for Phase Changes:** Melting, boiling, condensation, and freezing all involve latent heat — energy changes temperature without changing it (energy goes into breaking or forming molecular bonds).

6. **Use Everyday Analogies:** Relating unfamiliar problems to common experiences (cooking, weather, clothing) strengthens your reasoning.
